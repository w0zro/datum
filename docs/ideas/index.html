Designing datum

A light and dark colorscheme derived from color science rather than taste.

A colorscheme has two hard jobs and one easy one. The easy job is looking nice in a screenshot. The hard jobs are being legible for hours — across two backgrounds, for eyes that differ — and meaning something, using color to encode structure rather than smearing it around as decoration. Most schemes are tuned by nudging hex values until a sample file looks right on the author's monitor. This one is derived from published research, and every color below has a reason and a measured number attached to it. This page is styled with the palette it documents; toggle the theme to see both.

1The research

Eleven findings drive the design. The first six are about legibility; the rest are about which colors and how loud.

1.1  Design in a perceptually uniform space — OKLCH

sRGB and HSL are perceptually non-uniform: two colors with the same HSL "lightness" can differ wildly in apparent brightness. Björn Ottosson's OKLCH (2020) separates Lightness, Chroma and Hue so that equal L looks equally bright. Give a set of accents the same L and they read as a family. → We design every color in OKLCH and export sRGB hex.

1.2  Keep hue identity, mirror lightness — the Solarized lesson

Ethan Schoonover's Solarized was built in CIELAB with symmetric lightness relationships, so switching modes preserves the perceived contrast between tones, and its accents keep fixed hues across both modes. → A color's hue angle is identical in light and dark mode; only its lightness and chroma change per background.

1.3  Dark backgrounds are not black; light backgrounds are not white

Pure #000 with light text causes halation — a haloing that readers with astigmatism experience as smearing — plus harsh fatigue; the field standard is a soft near-black around #121212. Pure #fff is a glare source. → Dark bg0 sits at OKLCH L 0.18, light bg0 at L 0.97; both carry a faint cool bias — a chosen neutral.

1.4  A dark theme is not an inverted light theme

Two perceptual effects break naive inversion. Simultaneous contrast makes the same swatch look dimmer on near-black than on white. The Bezold–Brücke hue shift drifts perceived hue as luminance changes. The practical rule: dark-mode colors need a chroma boost of roughly 15–25 % and sometimes a hue nudge to feel equivalent. → Dark accents are re-derived at their own L/C, not the light colors re-plotted.

1.5  Contrast — WCAG 2.2 as the floor, APCA as the gauge

The WCAG 2.x ratio (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large/UI) is the accessibility floor, but its maths is unreliable near black — exactly the dark-mode regime. APCA (candidate method for WCAG 3) reports a perceptual lightness-contrast Lc that means the same thing across the whole range and treats dark-on-light and light-on-dark differently, as the eye does. → Every foreground clears WCAG 4.5:1 and is tuned by APCA (target Lc ≥ 60 for frequent text, ≥ 45 for accents and comments).

1.6  Positive polarity is the sharper mode

Piepenbrock, Mayr & Buchner found a consistent positive-polarity advantage: people read faster and proofread more accurately with dark text on a light background, at every age, because a light background keeps the pupil constricted — increasing depth of field and reducing the impact of astigmatism. Yet dark mode is preferred in dim light and eases the halation glow. There is no universal winner. → Light mode is the acuity mode, not an afterthought; both get first-class tuning.

1.7  Which hues read best flips with the background

On dark grounds the high-luminance hues (yellow, green, cyan) read best while saturated blue and violet read worst; on white it inverts — blue, red and violet read well, and yellow reads worst because its high luminance leaves too little contrast. This is luminance, not taste. → It is why the two modes can't share colors and why light-mode "yellow" must become a dark olive. Frequent tokens skew warm/high-luminance in dark, cool in light.

1.8  Saturated blue text is hard to focus — chromostereopsis

Because the eye refracts short wavelengths more, blue focuses slightly in front of the retina and red behind it, so saturated blue text sits at the wrong focal plane and a saturated red beside a saturated blue produces a depth shimmer. The fixes are to desaturate and to avoid adjacency. → Keyword-blue is kept gently desaturated, and a saturated error-red never butts directly against it.

1.9  A data-derived hue set — Okabe–Ito

Okabe & Ito's eight-color qualitative palette (popularized by Bang Wong in Nature Methods, 2011) is the de facto scientific standard: engineered from the start to stay distinct under protanopia, deuteranopia and tritanopia, with deliberately distinct luminance per hue. → Rather than pick arbitrary evenly-spaced hues, datum anchors its hue angles on the Okabe–Ito set and moves only L/C per background. Its hues map almost one-to-one onto our roles.

the mapping

blue → keywords · orange → numbers/values · bluish-green → strings · reddish-purple → constants & function definitions · vermillion → errors. Types take a teal placed between string-green and keyword-blue, because Okabe–Ito's sky-blue sits only 8° from its blue and the two collapse once both are dark enough for white-background contrast (finding 1.7).

1.10  Color intensity should be inverse to token frequency

Early work (Baecker & Gilmore) found color cues could improve readability up to ~25 %, but practice since (Alabaster; tonsky's everyone is getting syntax highlighting wrong) observes that if everything is highlighted, nothing is. The usual reading of that — "color fewer tokens" — throws away color many people genuinely enjoy staring at all day. But the underlying principle isn't about the presence of color, it's about intensity: a token's loudness should be inverse to how often it appears. That is satisfiable with saturation instead of on/off. → Chroma-tiering. Every token carries a hue, but intensity encodes frequency. The sparse reference points (strings, numbers, constants, types, definitions) run at full chroma — saturated mid-tones. The ubiquitous glue (variables, function calls, parameters, operators — ~75 % of the text) shares those same hues but is pushed to a lightness near the foreground: that both separates it from its full-chroma sibling on the CVD-safe lightness channel (§1.11) and softens it to a pastel, because the sRGB gamut narrows near white. Pure punctuation and comments stay neutral; keywords are colored but not bold. The screen reads as vividly colored, yet the eye still gets a loud/quiet hierarchy.

1.11  Color-vision deficiency is not an edge case

Some form of color-vision deficiency affects roughly 8 % of men and 0.5 % of women — the common red-green types are X-linked recessive, which is why prevalence skews male, but it is everyone's concern, not a men-only one. The blue↔orange axis survives it, and lightness differences survive every kind. → CVD-aware: keep the full hue range, but put the load-bearing contrast on blue/orange and separate any two co-occurring token colors by lightness. This is also exactly what lets the two chroma tiers (§1.10) coexist: a glue color and its full-chroma sibling share a hue, so only the lightness gap tells them apart — and lightness is the one channel no CVD collapses. Validated by simulating all three CVD types (Machado et al. 2009).

2The derivation pipeline

The palette is generated by a script (tools/derive.py, pure stdlib), not hand-picked, so the numbers below are reproducible.

  1. Neutrals first. Fix dark bg0 at L 0.18 and light at L 0.97, then derive the tonal ramp at mirrored lightness steps so both modes share a contrast structure.
  2. Anchor the accent hues once on the Okabe–Ito angles (§1.9); these are shared by both modes.
  3. Tune lightness & chroma per background. Dark accents high-L and chroma-boosted; light accents low-L to clear 4.5:1 on the off-white. Co-occurring pairs are pushed apart in L for CVD; blue stays gently desaturated (§1.8). These are tier 1.
  4. Derive the glue tier. For each glue role, take its full-chroma sibling's hue and push the lightness toward the foreground — high in dark, low in light — yielding a quieter tone that (a) reads quiet, (b) separates from the sibling on the CVD-safe channel, and (c) as body text keeps the higher contrast. This is tier 2.
  5. Verify & iterate. Compute WCAG and APCA for every pair, simulate the three CVD types over all eleven colors, and adjust L/C — never the shared hue anchors — until the targets pass.
  6. Quantize to cterm-256 for terminals without truecolor, noting where the approximation diverges.

Every one of the eleven syntax colors clears WCAG 4.5:1 in both modes; light orange at 4.5 is the tightest. The two tiers invert their lightness by mode: in dark the glue is a bright pastel and the accents are saturated mid-tones; in light the glue goes dark (it is the body text, so it should hold the higher contrast) while the accents stay mid. And the modes are genuinely different colors, not inversions — light green is a deep pine, dark green a bright mint, same 165° hue.

4Color-vision-deficiency validation

Each CVD type is simulated (Machado 2009, severity 1.0) and we measure the minimum pairwise Oklab distance across all eleven syntax colors (both tiers) — how close the two most similar colors become. Larger is safer.

dark0.0730.0390.0470.025
light0.0580.0070.0200.016

Read honestly — this is the real cost of maximal color. Eleven colors on seven CVD-safe hues is a crowded wheel: the worst-case separation is far tighter than a restrained palette would be (a pre-tier version of datum cleared 0.076 under deuteranopia; this clears 0.047). Two things keep it honest. First, the tier split rides lightness, which no CVD collapses, so a glue color never merges with its own full-chroma sibling — the loud/quiet distinction always survives. Second, the tightest residual pairs are low-stakes: in light mode orange↔yellow (numbers vs constants) compress to 0.007 under protanopia, but a numeric literal and a SCREAMING_CASE constant are never confusable by anything but color. The high-frequency, high-stakes distinctions — comment vs code, string vs everything, keyword vs variable — all ride the blue/green/neutral axis, which stays clear under the common red-green types. This is a deliberate trade of headroom for color; the restrained palette is one config change away for anyone who wants it back.

5.1  Two dependencies worth stating plainly

The tiers need Tree-sitter. Base Vim regex syntax cannot tell a function definition from a call, and never populates @variable, @operator, or @punctuation at all. Under regex highlighting the quiet tier simply doesn't appear — calls, variables and operators fall back to fg0. Tree-sitter's captures (@function.call vs @function, etc.) are what make the loud/quiet split real, so datum is authored against them and the config starts Tree-sitter wherever a parser exists.

Operators earn their color on evidence. They are frequent, so §1.10 would leave them quiet — which the quiet tier does. But the one replicable benefit of coloring a token class is targeted visual search: color gives no speed-up when the target's color is unknown, but a considerable one when it is known and consistent (Sarkar 2015; Beelders & du Plessis eye-tracking). A stable, quiet operator color turns "find the assignment / the comparison / the pipeline" into a color-pop search without adding noise — exactly what a quiet tone in the keyword-blue family delivers.

6Reproducing & changing the palette

The script tools/derive.py (python3 tools/derive.py) is the source of truth for these numbers. To change a color: adjust its OKLCH L/C (or, rarely, the shared hue anchor) in the script, re-run the verification, confirm the §1.5 targets and §4 CVD separation still hold, then update colors/datum.vim, the tables in §3, and the CVD table in §4 in the same change. This document is only trustworthy if it moves in lockstep with the palette.
